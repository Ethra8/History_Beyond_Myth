ISLE of aBALONE

Abalones, the Sea Truffles

The species Haliotis tuberculata is also called ormer or sea-ear, and is an edible marine gastropod mollusc, that has an ear-shaped shell perforated with holes and occurs near the Channel Islands 1Reference: Collins Dictionary.

This type of abalone native to the British Isles is one of our forgotten delicacies. The name ormer comes from the French oreille de mer. The French prize them highly and also call them the 'truffles of the sea' 2Reference: In search of Britain's forgotten seafood delicacy - deliciousmagazine.co.uk 3Reference: L'ormeau, la Truffe des Mers - passionsante.be .

Abalones have been a luxury product since antiquity, both for their iridescent mother-of-pearl surface 4Reference: Investigative Conservation of Anglo-Saxon finds from Bloodmoore Hill, Carlton Colville, Suffolk - historicengland.org.uk used in luxurious jewelry, as well as their meat-like flesh, still used in the finest gastronomy 5Reference: Truffles of the Sea - abalonebretagne.com .

The Mists of the Isle of Guernsey

Considered a quintessential delicacy in the Channel Island of Guernsey, abalones have been traditionally collected on the island for centuries6Reference: Guernsey Ormers - tasteatlas.com .

The Isle of Guernsey is situated in the English Channel, half-way between Brittany and Southern Wales. This favours intense mists around its coastline, which is also sorrounded by sharp rocks, as appreciated in the picture.

The shallow seabed sorrounding the island, with peaky and sharp rocks that step far into the sea, is a valuable natural defence, making it fairly difficult to access the island by boat. Land is only accessible through very few narrow points where, by sorting the cutting rocks as a deathly laberynth, a small boat can eventually reach to shore on a couple of very small beaches.
